Answering Inappropriate Questions

♪ [music] ♪ Let's talk about inappropriate questions. Unfortunately, you probably will encounter interviewers who ask questions that they shouldn't. Many interviewers are untrained or just plain awkward. The problem is that the candidate is often the one left feeling uncomfortable and wondering whether to answer the question or risk awkwardness by declining?


[00:00:29]


In the worst cases you might get questions that seem to be probing for information that can be used to illegally discriminate against you. Remember, interviewers should only be asking questions related to your ability to do your job, not your personal life. Sometimes these questions are asked innocently, as part of getting to know you, and sometimes they're asked because the interviewer has biases and is looking to exclude candidates for unfair reasons. You should be aware of what types of questions are inappropriate and why.


[00:00:58]


This will also help you decide if and how you want to answer. Let's start with the basics here. There are federal and state laws on the books that prohibit employers from discriminating against candidates on the basis of certain qualities that have nothing to do with your ability to do the job. Now, laws vary from state to state, but these factors generally include marital status, family obligations, including whether you have children or other caretaking responsibilities, national origin or race, age, gender, sexual preference, and any disabilities or health conditions.


[00:01:30]


Well-trained interviewers will not ask questions related to these topics, because they don't want to put the company at risk of discriminating or being accused of discriminating. It's not illegal to ask these questions. What's illegal is to base your hiring decision on the information. Let's look at a few examples of inappropriate questions that are often asked by interviewers who don't know any better, and maybe some who do. "What kind of name is Skillings?" Skillings is my last name, and I've been asked about it many


[00:01:59]


times by people who are just making conversation, but it's not something you should be asked in a job interview. Interviewers cannot discriminate against candidates based on where they're from, or where their family is from. "Do you have any kids?" This is clearly not an appropriate question. In a worst-case scenario, the interviewer may be looking to avoid candidates with more personal responsibilities, assuming that someone with kids won't be as willing to put in as many hours. "Oh, I went to Columbia, too. When did you graduate?" Most likely, this one is an innocent question and an opportunity for bonding,


[00:02:30]


but it does touch on the subject of age. They should not be probing into your exact age, though it can be hard to preserve much mystery when you have to submit a resume and show your face in the interview. Unfortunately, age discrimination against older candidates is all too common. You don't have to reveal your exact age, especially if you've gone to great pains to downplay that you're over 50 on your resume. These were just a few examples. Be on the lookout for any and all others that similarly cross the line from professional to personal.


[00:03:00]


Now, let's talk about how you should handle it, if faced with an inappropriate question. Often, this comes down to a judgement call. If you feel it was an innocent mistake, if it seems that the interviewer was innocently making conversation, it's probably in your best interest just to deflect the question. If you can, steer the conversation toward a more neutral topic. For example, with the classic inappropriate question like, "Where are you from originally?" you could answer, "Oh, I consider myself a New Yorker. I've lived here a long time."


[00:03:29]


On the other hand, if the interviewer seems to be purposely probing for irrelevant information, that's a red flag. Now, just because one interviewer is shady doesn't mean that the whole organization is corrupt, but perk up your ears if you're getting a bad feeling. In cases like this, you can politely decline to answer the question. You could gently deflect back to more appropriate topics, something like, "I don't think that's too relevant, but I would love to tell you more about my programming experience." Keep in mind that unfortunately this may hurt your chances of getting the job.


[00:03:58]


At minimum, it's going to create an awkward moment. Hopefully the interviewer will realize their mistake and just move on to better topics. However, if an interviewer is going to judge you based on not answering a blatantly inappropriate question, then most likely this isn't someone that you want to work for, anyway. So, don't fear the inappropriate question. They're becoming less common, as more organizations wise-up to training their managers on appropriate interview techniques, but now, after this lesson, you'll never be caught off-guard if you do run into an awkward interview situation.
